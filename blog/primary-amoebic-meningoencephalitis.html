Primary Amoebic Meningoencephalitis (PAM)

By Dr. Subrahmanyam Karuturi • June 20, 2026 • English

Table of Contents

  • 2. The Organism: Naegleria Fowleri
  • 3. Symptoms & Clinical Progression
  • 4. Warm Freshwater Swimming Safety

1. What is Primary Amoebic Meningoencephalitis?

Primary Amoebic Meningoencephalitis (PAM) is a rare, acute, and rapidly fatal central nervous system infection. The condition leads to severe inflammation and destruction of brain tissue (necrosis), mimicking bacterial meningitis in its initial stages.

2. The Organism: Naegleria Fowleri

The infection is caused by Naegleria fowleri, a thermophilic, free-living amoeba. It thrives in warm freshwater environments like lakes, rivers, ponds, and poorly chlorinated swimming pools. It enters the body when contaminated water is forced up the nasal passages, where it travels along the olfactory nerve into the brain.

"Importantly, Naegleria fowleri cannot infect you through drinking water; it must enter through the nasal mucosa."

3. Symptoms & Clinical Progression

Symptoms typically develop 1 to 9 days after nasal exposure. The initial stage mimics bacterial meningitis and includes:

  • Severe frontal headache
  • High fever and chills
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Stiff neck

As the infection progresses, patients experience confusion, loss of balance, seizures, hallucinations, and rapid coma. The disease progresses extremely fast, with death usually occurring within 5 to 12 days of symptom onset.

4. Warm Freshwater Swimming Safety

To reduce risk of infection, especially during hot summer months in fresh waterbodies, apply these safety parameters:

  • Avoid jumping or diving into warm freshwater ponds or stagnant rivers.
  • Wear nasal clips or pinch your nose shut when swimming in open freshwaters.
  • Do not disturb the silty sediment at the bottom of shallow pools where amoebae reside.
  • Use only sterile, distilled, or boiled water for nasal irrigation systems (such as Neti pots).
